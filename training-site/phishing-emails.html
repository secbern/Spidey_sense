How to evaluate the true sender (emails) The display name (e.g. "PayPal" or "Your Bank") can be set to anything — it is not proof of who sent the email. Always check the actual email address (the part after the angle brackets or in the "From" field when expanded). On phone/tablet, tap the display name to see the real address. On desktop, open the message and look for "From" details or "Show original" / "View source" to see the real sender. Also check Reply-To if present — a reply might go to a different, fake address. For links, hover (or long-press) to see the real URL before clicking; the visible text can say "paypal.com" while the link goes elsewhere.

Example 1: Fake "account alert"

Training example only. All addresses and links are defanged.

From: PayPal Security <noreply@paypa1-account[.]com>
Subject: Action required: verify your account

Dear customer,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. To avoid suspension, please verify your identity within 24 hours.

Log in here to verify

If you did not request this, please ignore this message.

— PayPal

Why this is suspicious Display name says "PayPal Security" but the real address is noreply@paypa1-account[.]com — a 1 instead of L, and "paypa1-account" is not PayPal's domain. Real PayPal uses addresses like @paypal.com. The link would go to a fake site; hover to see the real URL.

Example 2: Fake "delivery" or "document"

Training example only.

From: Amazon <ship@amaz0n-orders[.]support>
Subject: Your package could not be delivered

We tried to deliver your order but the address was incorrect.

Confirm your details and reschedule: Update delivery

Thank you,
Amazon

Why this is suspicious The real sender is ship@amaz0n-orders[.]support — zero instead of O, and the domain ends in .support, not .com. Amazon's real emails come from @amazon.com (or similar). Never enter payment or address details from a link in an email; go to the site by typing amazon.com yourself.

Example 3: Urgent "boss" or "IT" request

Training example only.

From: David Chen (CEO) <d.chen@company-internal[.]xyz>
Subject: Urgent – need gift cards today

Hi, I'm in a meeting and can't talk. I need you to buy $500 in Apple gift cards and send me the codes as soon as possible. Reply to this email with the codes.

Thanks,
David

Why this is suspicious Legitimate work requests don't come as "buy gift cards and send codes." The sender address d.chen@company-internal[.]xyz uses a generic .xyz domain that a real company would not use for internal email. Verify by calling or messaging the person through a known channel (not replying to this email).
Quick checklist: evaluating the true sender (1) Ignore the display name; look at the actual email address after the < > or in "From" details. (2) Check if the domain (the part after @) is really the company's — no typos, no .xyz/.top/.click instead of .com. (3) Hover over links to see the real URL before clicking. (4) Don't reply or click when the message is urgent and asks for login, payment, or gift cards; go to the real site or contact the person another way.
