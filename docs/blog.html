Personal Cybersecurity Plan

The Bad News

Our digital economy is an increasingly scary and confusing place. Spam email—always annoying—has become endemic. Websites now contain multiple pop ups and videos constantly playing to grab our attention. And that’s just on the laptop—which we can close and walk away from. Spam texts and calls never give us respite on our phones, leading us to either ignore ALL calls at risk of missing an important one, or answering the calls just to have to play FBI profiler on the phone. And then of course there’s the mail. Envelope after envelope of data breach notifications—It’s all too much!

The Good News

You can improve your security. There’s a saying—you don’t need to be faster than the bear—you just need to be faster than the other guy. This holds true for most cyber threats. They are opportunistic and don’t want to put in any more effort than necessary. If you increase your security with the following steps, you will remove most of the risk.

First—create time in your schedule to work on security. Think of it like cleaning the house or working on finances. Set aside an hour or so to dedicate to it per week. Computers may be fast but the internet takes time.

Ok now let’s start.

Step 1: Account Inventory

Make a list of your online accounts, and group them into these categories below. Don’t worry if you can’t remember them all—just try to get the important ones first.

  • Core Accounts – Banking / Investments / Savings (Where the money IS)
  • Core Services – Credit Cards / Healthcare / Insurance / Mortgage / Taxes / Utilities / Amazon (Where the money goes—to things I must pay for)
  • Other Services – Newspaper Subscriptions / Random Accounts / Charities

Step 2: Passwords

The most important thing to avoid with passwords (we’ll get to two-factor) is PASSWORD RE-USE. Don’t use the same passwords for two or more different accounts, and definitely don’t re-use a password that is shared with a Core Assets account. Passwords are stolen all the time and any memorable or long-standing one can be considered possibly compromised. They are simply the first barrier for bad guys—but not a super effective one.

So, having a simple password like “P@ssword1” is a bad practice, but you also don’t need to have a super-spy-200-character-password that you’re never going to remember. Why? Because you’ll forget it and in a rush, reset it to something easier. So make it long, but not insane. Here’s a good comic about this: https://xkcd.com/936/

And if you use a password manager (see below), you won’t need to generate your own anyway.

Option 1: Password manager

Download a password manager such as LastPass or 1Password. Here’s a Wired article about them: https://www.wired.com/story/best-password-managers/ However, please understand that this will require some level of tech understanding for you to properly use. If you don’t feel comfortable or have someone who can help you with this, totally fine. Move on to #2.

Option 2: A physical (gasp!) book

It is 100% ok to keep a password book. I do! Keep it in a secure location, like a drawer in your office or a locked cabinet. Don’t make it obvious and write “PASSWORDS” on it, and generally it should not leave this secure location. If you MUST take it with you when traveling, don’t put it in the same bag as your computer.

What I do: I use a password manager and then keep the Master Password for my password manager in a physical book in my house.

Now, make sure that your passwords for Core Assets and Core Services are all unique. If you need to change them to do so, change them and write them down in your password manager or your password book.

Step 3: Multi-factor authentication

For all your Core Assets accounts, go to the provider’s website and ensure you are enrolled in 2-factor authentication (2FA). This can be a bit tricky so make sure you have time.

First, check out this directory of which services do and do not support 2FA: https://2fa.directory/us/?noCache=1

If one of your core assets or services accounts does not support 2FA—you should look into switching providers.

Next, log into your accounts one by one—starting with the most important—and do the following:

Go into your account settings and somewhere under “Security” options, look for Multi-Factor Authentication, or Two Factor Authentication. You will want to enroll.

SMS-based 2-factor

This is where the site will send you a text code to your phone. This is the least secure method of 2-factor, but it is better than nothing. If this is all they offer, do this.

Authentication app

This is where you can use a free app like Google Authenticator to enroll in 2FA. This is usually the best way for most people to do 2FA. Here’s a link on how to do it: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eVnf3x5w4rA

Physical security token

This is the most secure method of authentication, where the organization will send you an actual device which contains your codes. This can of course be inconvenient—if you lose or forget the device, you’ll be locked out. If you are confident you can manage this, go for it.

Now that you have your Core Assets under secure 2 Factor Authentication, you can safely go and re-set your Core Services (do 2FA again if you can, and save the password in your password manager) and then at your leisure, secure your Other Services.

Whew. You’ve done it! Now your accounts are more secure than they were before.
