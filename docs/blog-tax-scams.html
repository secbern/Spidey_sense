Tax Season Scams: Warning Signs, Prevention & Recovery

Tax season is no longer just a deadline—it’s a prime time for scammers. Criminals use stolen Social Security numbers and other personal data to file fake tax returns, claim refunds, or pressure people into paying “owed” taxes that don’t exist. This article covers how tax-related fraud works, how to spot it, and what to do if you’re affected.

What Is Tax Identity Theft?

Tax identity theft happens when someone uses your personal information (especially your Social Security number or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) to file a fraudulent tax return and claim a refund in your name. They often file early in the season so the refund is issued before you file your real return. When you try to file, the IRS rejects it because a return has already been submitted under your SSN.

For victims, the impact isn’t only financial. According to the IRS Taxpayer Advocate Service, identity theft cases take an average of about 22 months to resolve—nearly two years of delayed refunds and uncertainty.

Warning Signs You May Be a Victim

  • Your return is rejected because the IRS says one has already been filed with your SSN. This is how most people first find out.
  • You get IRS notice CP5071 or similar, asking you to verify your identity.
  • W-2s or 1099s from employers or payers you don’t recognize.
  • Unexpected refund payments or notices about taxes for years when you didn’t file.
  • Tax transcripts you never requested.

If a criminal has enough of your data to file a tax return, they may also try to open credit cards, take out loans, or commit medical or employment fraud. Treat tax fraud as a sign to lock down your identity and credit.

Fake “IRS” Contact: How Scammers Pressure You

Scammers often pretend to be the IRS to scare people into paying fake debts or handing over personal information. It’s important to know how the real IRS communicates:

  • The IRS does not demand immediate payment by gift cards, wire transfer, or digital payment apps.
  • The IRS does not initiate contact by unsolicited email, text, or social media to ask for personal or financial details.
  • The IRS typically contacts by mail first. They may eventually call, but they won’t threaten arrest, deportation, or license revocation if you don’t pay on the spot.

Any message that creates panic and urges you to pay or “verify” right away is a red flag. When in doubt, hang up or don’t reply, and contact the IRS yourself using the official number or website (irs.gov).

Recovering from Tax Identity Theft

If you believe someone filed a fraudulent return using your information, act quickly:

  1. Respond to any IRS notices and follow the instructions to verify your identity.
  2. File a paper return if your e-file was rejected, and include Form 14039, the Identity Theft Affidavit.
  3. Once the IRS confirms the fraud, they can issue an Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN)—a six-digit code you’ll need for future filings. This greatly reduces the chance of someone filing again in your name.
  4. Check your credit reports, dispute unfamiliar accounts, and consider placing a credit freeze. A freeze limits who can access your credit file and helps prevent new accounts from being opened in your name.

Be alert for follow-up scams. After a data breach or identity theft, criminals may target you again with fake “recovery” or “refund” offers. The IRS will not ask you to confirm your refund via gift cards, wire, or unsolicited email links.

Prevention: Reduce Your Risk Year-Round

Tax fraud isn’t only a once-a-year issue. Stolen SSNs and other data are used whenever it’s convenient for the criminal. You can lower your risk by:

  • Filing early when possible, so a fraudster has less time to file before you.
  • Using an Identity Protection PIN if the IRS offers you one (e.g., after resolving identity theft or through the IRS IP PIN program).
  • Keeping tax documents and SSNs secure—shred old paperwork, and don’t share your SSN by email or on unsecured sites.
  • Being cautious with email and links that claim to be from the IRS or your tax preparer. When in doubt, go to irs.gov or your preparer’s site by typing the URL or using a saved bookmark.
