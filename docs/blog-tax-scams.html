When “IRS” Isn’t the IRS: Tax Scams and How to Fight Back

Every year, crooks use tax season as a cover. They send fake “IRS” emails and texts, or they use stolen Social Security numbers to file returns in someone else’s name and pocket the refund. The damage can drag on for a long time—experts like those at Cyber Insurance News point out that tax identity theft cases often take close to two years to fully resolve. Here’s how to tell what’s real, what’s not, and what to do if you get hit.

The “Pay Now or Else” Trap

Lots of tax scams work the same way: someone claims to be the IRS and says you owe money or must “verify” your identity right now. They might threaten arrest, license suspension, or a lost refund. The goal is to make you act before you think.

Real IRS behavior is different. The agency does not ask for payment by gift cards, wire transfer, Zelle, Venmo, or any similar method. It does not first reach out by unsolicited email, text, or social media to demand money or personal details. Serious notices usually show up in the mail. If you get a call or message that feels high-pressure and wants payment on the spot, treat it as a scam. Hang up or don’t reply, then look up the real IRS contact info yourself (irs.gov or the number on a letter you actually received).

Someone Else Filing Under Your Name

Another kind of tax fraud has nothing to do with fake emails. A thief uses your SSN (or ITIN) to file a return and grab the refund before you do. You might not notice until you sit down to file and the system tells you a return has already been submitted for your Social Security number. That “already filed” message is one of the most common ways people find out they’ve been victimized.

Other clues: IRS letters you didn’t expect (for example, a CP5071 or similar asking you to verify your identity), W-2s or 1099s from employers or payers you don’t know, or notices about refunds or balances for years you didn’t file. If your data was enough for someone to file a return, they may also try credit fraud, loans, or other identity crimes—so treat a tax problem as a signal to lock down your identity and credit.

If It Happens to You

Don’t wait. Open and follow any instructions in real IRS notices. If your e-file is rejected because a return was already filed, you’ll need to file on paper and attach Form 14039 (Identity Theft Affidavit). After the IRS confirms the fraud, they can give you an Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN)—a six-digit code you’ll use on future returns. That makes it much harder for anyone to file again in your name.

Separately, pull your credit reports, dispute anything you don’t recognize, and think about putting a credit freeze in place. A freeze blocks new accounts from being opened in your name unless you temporarily lift it. Watch out for a second wave of scams: after a breach or identity theft, bad actors sometimes follow up with fake “recovery” or “refund” messages. The real IRS will not ask you to confirm a refund by clicking a link in an email or paying with gift cards.

Staying Ahead of the Game

Tax-related fraud isn’t limited to January–April. Stolen data gets used whenever it’s useful. Filing as early as you can (when you have what you need) reduces the window for someone else to file first. If you’re eligible for an IP PIN, use it. Keep tax papers and your SSN out of insecure places—shred what you don’t need, and don’t send your SSN by email or through sketchy sites. For any link or message that says it’s from the IRS or your preparer, don’t trust the link: go to irs.gov or your preparer’s real site by typing the address or using a bookmark.
