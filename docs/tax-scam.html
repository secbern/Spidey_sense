In this scenario (tax scams): Scammers pretend to be the IRS or a tax service to steal money or personal data. The real IRS does not demand payment by gift cards, wire transfer, or payment apps. They do not first contact you by unsolicited email, text, or social media. They do not threaten arrest or license revocation if you don’t pay immediately. If you’re unsure, hang up or don’t click—and contact the IRS yourself at irs.gov or the phone number on an official letter you received.

Example 1: Fake “IRS” demand for immediate payment

Training example only. All addresses and links are defanged.

From: IRS.gov Alerts <no-reply@irs-gov-refund[.]com>
Subject: URGENT – Final notice: Pay now or face legal action

Dear Taxpayer,

Your account has an outstanding balance of $2,847.32. You have 48 HOURS to pay or we will:

  • Issue a warrant for your arrest
  • Suspend your driver's license
  • Place a lien on your property

To resolve this immediately, pay using one of these methods:

• Apple Gift Cards – buy at any store and send the codes to us
• Wire transfer to the account in the link below
• Zelle or Venmo to the number we will provide when you call

Click here to pay now and avoid legal action

Do not ignore this notice. Call us immediately: 1-800-XXX-XXXX.

— Internal Revenue Service

Why this is suspicious The real IRS never demands payment by gift cards, Zelle, or Venmo. They don’t threaten arrest or license suspension in an email, and they don’t first contact you by unsolicited email. The sender address is irs-gov-refund[.]com—not irs.gov. Real IRS correspondence usually comes by mail first. This is a classic tax scam: urgency + fake payment methods + fake sender.

Example 2: Fake “refund” or “verify identity” email

Training example only.

From: IRS Refund Center <refunds@tax-refund-verify[.]net>
Subject: Your refund of $1,240 is ready – confirm your details

Good news! We have processed your tax refund.

To receive your $1,240.00 refund, you must verify your identity and banking information within 72 hours. Click the link below and enter your Social Security number, date of birth, and bank account or debit card details. Refunds are issued within 24 hours after verification.

Verify my refund and get my payment

If you do not verify, your refund will be forfeited.

— IRS Refund Department

Why this is suspicious The IRS does not send unsolicited emails asking you to “verify” your identity or bank details by clicking a link. Refunds are not “forfeited” if you don’t respond to an email. The real sender is tax-refund-verify[.]net—not irs.gov. This type of message is designed to steal your SSN, date of birth, and bank information. Real refund status is checked at irs.gov (by typing it yourself or using a bookmark), not via links in email.

Example 3: Fake “IRS” text message

Training example only.

IRS: Your tax refund has been blocked due to suspicious activity. Verify your identity now to release your refund: irs-verify[.]com/refund

Reply STOP to opt out.

Why this is suspicious The IRS does not send refund alerts or identity verification requests by unsolicited text. The link goes to irs-verify[.]com—not irs.gov. This is a phishing text to steal your personal and financial information. Do not click; check your refund status only at the real irs.gov site.
Quick checklist: tax scam red flags (1) Payment method – Real IRS doesn’t ask for gift cards, wire, Zelle, or Venmo. (2) First contact – Real IRS typically contacts by mail first, not unsolicited email or text. (3) Urgency and threats – Arrest, license suspension, or “refund forfeited in 72 hours” are pressure tactics. (4) Sender and links – Check the real email address and URL; irs.gov is the only official IRS domain. When in doubt, go to irs.gov yourself—don’t click links in messages.
