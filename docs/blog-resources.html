Free resources: reporting fraud & staying protected

Below are free, easy-to-use resources for the average person: where to report fraud if you think you’re a victim, and what protective measures major banks and retirement services typically offer to help keep you safe.

1. Where to report fraud if you suspect you are a victim

Reporting helps law enforcement spot patterns and sometimes recover funds. These sites are free and run by the government.

General scams and fraud

  • ReportFraud.ftc.gov – Run by the Federal Trade Commission. Report scams, fake businesses, and bad practices here. Your report is shared with thousands of law enforcement agencies. The FTC can’t fix your individual case but uses reports to investigate and warn others.
  • IC3.gov (Internet Crime Complaint Center) – Run by the FBI. Use this for online and cyber-related crime (phishing, fake websites, online fraud). Reports help the FBI track and investigate; in some cases they can help freeze stolen funds.

Identity theft

  • IdentityTheft.gov – FTC site just for identity theft. It walks you step-by-step through what to do (place a fraud alert, get a credit freeze, report to the IRS if needed, etc.).

Financial products and services

  • Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) – Submit a complaint – For problems with banks, credit cards, loans, and other financial services. The CFPB forwards complaints to the company and tracks responses.

If you already paid a scammer

Act quickly based on how you paid:

  • Credit or debit card – Call your card issuer (the number on the back of the card) and say you want to dispute a fraudulent charge. Many issuers have $0 liability for unauthorized use if you report promptly.
  • Bank transfer – Contact your bank right away to report unauthorized debits. They may be able to reverse or limit the damage.
  • Wire (e.g. Western Union, MoneyGram) – Contact the wire service immediately to try to cancel or recover; also report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.
  • Money app (Venmo, Zelle, Cash App, etc.) – Report to the app and to your linked bank or card issuer. Reversals are often harder with these, so reporting quickly matters.

No matter how you paid, also file a report at ReportFraud.ftc.gov so the FTC has a record and can use it for investigations.

2. What protective measures do major banks and retirement services provide?

Banks and retirement providers (like Fidelity, Vanguard, and others) offer several free tools. Turn these on and use them to stay safer.

Credit freezes and fraud alerts (free, for everyone)

These are free and don’t hurt your credit score:

  • Credit freeze – Stops anyone (including scammers) from opening new credit in your name. You place it with each of the three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). You can temporarily lift it when you need to apply for credit. FTC: Credit freezes and fraud alerts.
  • Fraud alert – Tells lenders to verify your identity before opening new credit. You set it with one bureau and it applies to all three. “Initial” fraud alert lasts 1 year; “extended” (after identity theft) can last 7 years.

What banks typically offer

  • Account and transaction alerts – Text or email when there’s a withdrawal, large purchase, or login from a new device. Turn these on in your bank’s app or website.
  • Two-factor authentication (2FA) – Extra step (code by text or app) when you sign in or send money. Strongly recommended.
  • Dispute process – If you didn’t authorize a charge, you can dispute it. Act quickly; rules and time limits vary by card and account type.
  • Card lock / freeze – Many banks let you temporarily lock your debit or credit card in the app if it’s lost or you see suspicious activity.

What retirement and investment providers typically offer

Companies like Fidelity, Vanguard, and similar brokerages usually provide:

  • Login and security alerts – Notifications for sign-ins, password changes, or withdrawals. Enable these in your account settings.
  • Two-factor authentication – Required or optional extra verification for logins and sometimes for moving money.
  • Transfer or withdrawal safeguards – Some let you restrict or lock certain types of transfers, or require extra verification for large withdrawals.
  • Fraud or customer protection guarantees – Many promise to reimburse you for losses from unauthorized activity if you followed their security steps (e.g. not sharing your password). Check your provider’s “security” or “protection guarantee” page for details.

Log into your accounts (using the real website or app, not a link from email) and look under Security, Alerts, or Profile to turn on these options. If you’re not sure, call the number on your statement or the provider’s official website.
